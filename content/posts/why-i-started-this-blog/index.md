I hesitated before starting this blog.

GPT writes faster than I do. It summarizes papers faster, explains APIs faster, generates tutorials faster, and rarely complains about being asked to rewrite the same paragraph five times. So the question is not simply "Why start a blog?" It is sharper than that:

Why spend time writing, when writing has become so cheap?

That hesitation is probably the real beginning of this blog.

## What No Longer Feels Worth Writing

There is a kind of technical writing that has lost most of its meaning: copying the official documentation, translating API usage, repackaging examples, or writing a post whose entire value is "here is the same thing, but in my words."

That used to be useful sometimes. Now it often feels unnecessary. If a blog post only moves information from one container to another, it is competing with official docs, search engines, and LLMs at the same time. That is not a fight I want to pick, partly because I would lose, and partly because winning would not be interesting.

So if this blog deserves to exist, it cannot exist as a warehouse of rewritten information.

It has to contain taste.

By taste, I do not mean style for its own sake. I mean the human residue left after practice: what I tried first, what failed, what looked elegant but was fragile, what sentence in a paper hid the real difficulty, what tool became useful only after I stopped using it the way the docs suggested. Taste is where experience leaves fingerprints.

That is the kind of writing I still want to read in the age of Gen AI. Not just correct text, but situated judgment.

## The PhD Problem

As everyone knows, or perhaps does not know, doing a PhD can be a remarkably repetitive and output-starved way to spend one's life.

Much of the work is quiet. You read, debug, revise, wait, fail, try again, and then discover that today's failure mainly proves yesterday's confidence was premature. The input is constant. The output is slow, uncertain, and often invisible for a long time.

That rhythm is part of research. I accept it. But I also find it dangerous to live inside pure input for too long. Thoughts that are never forced into shape become fog. Notes that never become explanations become storage. Reading without synthesis can feel productive while quietly making the mind heavier.

Writing is a buffer against that.

For me, one difference between work and a hobby is the balance between input and output. A good hobby gives you a way to make something, however small, and to see your own thinking take shape outside your head. This blog is an attempt to create that kind of output inside a life that is often dominated by input.

Not a grand project. More like a pressure valve.

## Why Writing, Especially Now

Gen AI has made polished text abundant. That is wonderful, and also a little unsettling.

When everyone can generate fluent summaries, tutorials, and explanations, the valuable part shifts. The scarce thing is no longer "a paragraph that sounds reasonable." The scarce thing is a person with enough contact with reality to say: this matters, this does not, this worked, this looked good but failed, this is the part the clean explanation usually hides.

That is why I think people need more distinctive, output-oriented hobbies now. Otherwise, our work and our self-expression start to smell a little too AI-flavored, do they not?

Writing a blog is one possible answer. It forces me to choose what I actually think. It asks me to explain, not merely collect. It makes my preferences, confusions, and standards visible. It also makes it harder to pretend I understood something just because I highlighted it.

## A Small Rule

I do not want this blog to become a place for vague reflection, motivational fog, or "ten things I learned" posts where none of the ten things has been tested by anything.

Empty talk does not deserve a place here.

The pieces worth keeping are the ones that survive contact with practice: a method that helped, a mistake that repeated often enough to deserve a name, a paper that changed how I look at a problem, a tool that became useful only after some friction, a conclusion that feels less fragile after being used.

In the age of AI, cheap generalities are everywhere. What remains valuable after the tide goes out is usually something narrower, more specific, and more earned.

That is the standard I want to hold myself to, even if I fail sometimes.

## Sharing, Not Preaching

I write to share, not to lecture.

I am not qualified to preach. More precisely, I do not think anyone is. The best writing I know does not stand above the reader. It says: here is what I saw from where I stood; maybe it helps you see your own problem more clearly.

I do not have a deep academic background, nor a serious foundation in writing. I am still learning, carefully and sometimes clumsily. If this blog becomes useful, it will not be because I speak from a high place. It will be because I leave a record of what I tried, what worked, what failed, and what I learned after the failure.

## Borrowed Shape

The style of this site is inspired by Lilian Weng's personal blog. She is one of the researchers I admire, and her writing has always struck me as clear, careful, and generous.

I think imitation is a reasonable place to begin. You borrow a shape, then slowly discover which parts do not fit your hand.

Over time, I hope this blog becomes more recognizably mine.

## About Me

Oh, and one more thing.

"My identity is rather complicated. I am a poet."

That line is from the film *Manchurian Tiger*. I am borrowing it here because it is funny, a little absurd, and strangely useful. It is also a good warning label for this blog: some posts will be technical, some will be practical, and some may contain a person trying to stay human while learning how to think.
